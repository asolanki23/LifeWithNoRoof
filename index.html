My Story

Nobody likes to hear about kids being homeless. No one wants to imagine and equate innocent faces with horrible facts.

It is easier to swallow the soft, syrupy fantasy than the hard, bitter truth.

Homeless children, however, do exist;

I exist.

I'm one of the forgotten ones the ones who people call dirty smelly and...

I don't know what else.

Ever since I was young, my mother has been suffering from addiction.

Although addiction was her adversary, any choice and result that resulted

that addiction, including the many times we were homeless,

emotionally affected me.

There was a lot of moving around trying to find a roof over our heads. We lived with family members at first until my mother

decided to move again, she figured we were a burden to them, and she didn't want to cause them any trouble.

Then we stayed for a few days with friends after moving to stay in shelters. It was hard to remain in shelters because it

meant we had to stay with others we did not know and trust.

The experience was awful, we had to sleep on the hard floor, it stank, and often because the shelters

were full, we were dismissed from the shelter, which meant sleeping on the streets.

Homeless Young People

30%

More than 30% of homeless individuals have a care background and so many
children are homeless and not covered by children's programs.

There is a legal right to safe accommodation for young adults who have been in the welfare system as infants or have become vulnerable until they turn 18. To prepare for their future, complete their education and make the transition to independent living, they should also receive wider support. However, despite these legal protections, funding cuts by local councils have resulted in the lack of housing and resources to young people, leaving already disadvantaged children homeless and at risk of violence, sexual assault, ill health, and crime.

No Hope

Being a kid and being homeless is not easy. If they look at you differently, you wonder
what others would think, so you keep it secret, buried away where no one knows.

I came to discover that the trouble with burying things and not noticing them, is that
they grow inside of you unchecked until they become so great that they have nowhere
to go but out.

This monster that was homelessness ate away at me and made me not only ashamed
but angry-angry at my mother, angry at myself, angry at the world,

and I

lashed

out in every way I knew how.

What I didn't know then was that I wasn't meant to be upset or embarrassed, and
I realize now is that just because I was homeless, I am no less than anyone. It
taught me a lot while staying in the shelters, like there are more people than just me
who understand the things I've been through.

People are there for all. Someone who, as we come into the community, helps us find
a place, meet new people and someone who supports us. To be honest, being
homeless now that I'm older and on my own is always something I deal with.

I'm always going from house to house and going from shelter to shelter. Although
I hope things will improve, I am helpless at times, trapped in the mentality that
things will stay the same.

I wish people would know that it's not an illness you catch to be homeless. It's not an
option either; one morning, you don't wake up and say,

"I think I want to be homeless starting today."
